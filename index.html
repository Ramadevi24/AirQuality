Health Recommendation

  • Wear Mask Its best to keep your windows closed

  • Stay Indoor Wear a Mask, if possible

  • Use Purifier Time to switch on your air purifiers

When the air quality index (AQI) hits orange, red, purple, or maroon levels, it's essential to take proactive measures to safeguard your well-being. Follow these guidelines to minimize the impact of elevated air pollution levels on your health.

Outdoor Precautions:
Reduce outdoor time to under 30 minutes during high AQI days and lower the intensity of outdoor activities. The longer and more strenuous the activity, the higher the risk of exposure to unhealthy air pollution levels, according to the EPA.

Mask Usage:
If outdoor exposure is unavoidable, consider wearing a mask. Note that not all masks provide equal protection against particle pollution. Cloth or dust masks may fall short, but well-fitted N95 or KN95 masks offer better filtration capabilities and can be beneficial on high AQI days.

Indoor Air Quality Maintenance:
Ensure healthy indoor air quality by keeping windows and doors closed . Opt for the recirculate setting on your air conditioning unit, use a portable HEPA air cleaner, or, in severe circumstances, create a clean room environment.

By adhering to these precautions, you can significantly reduce the impact of elevated air pollution on your health during days with high AQI levels. Stay informed and take proactive steps to create a healthier indoor and outdoor environment for yourself and your community.

Importance Of Air

Air is a mixture of gases compromising the earth’s atmosphere, it contains nitrogen, oxygen, and carbon dioxide which support the life of all living things on earth. Air also contains suspended particles such as dust pollen and pollution and the combination of gases and particles in the atmosphere plays a crucial role in supporting life, regulating climate, and influencing weather patterns. It is equally important for living organisms and has many applications. Living organisms depend on the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide to facilitate the inhaling and exhaling process, crucial not only for breathing but also for the production of energy through respiratory mechanisms. Additionally, nitrogen and carbon dioxide play vital roles in supporting plant life and their growth. Beyond sustaining life, air is instrumental in combustion, powering activities such as cooking, industrial processes, and transportation. It also contributes to temperature regulation by circulating hot and cold air, acting as a conductor of heat, and influencing the water cycle. Furthermore, air is a significant energy source, with oxygen being a key element for cellular respiration and the production of biochemical energy, essential for life maintenance. Despite its critical role, improper use of air, leading to air pollution, poses a threat to the environment, emphasizing the need for responsible environmental stewardship. The threat of air pollution looms large, reminding us of the urgent need for responsible environmental care. Let's cherish and protect this invisible wonder that breathes life into our world.

Understanding the AQI

Discover the air you breathe! Our Air Quality Index (AQI) follows the US EPA AQI classifications. Aligned with UAE's National Ambient Air Quality Standards, 100 points on the AQI table signify the threshold set by EAD to safeguard public health.

Keep an eye on the numbers: AQI values below 100 are typically deemed satisfactory. However, when the scale tips above 100, it's a signal that air quality is initially considered unhealthy for specific sensitive groups, gradually escalating to being unhealthy for everyone as the values rise. Stay informed for a breath of fresh air!

AQI Values Levels of Health Concern Colours
0-50 Good Green
51-100 Moderate Yellow
101-150 Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups Orange
151-200 Unhealthy Red
201-300 Very Unhealthy Purple
301-500 Hazardous Maroon

Dive into the nuances of air quality with the US EPA classifications:

Good (0-50): Your community enjoys satisfactory air quality, posing minimal to no risk.

Moderate (51-100): Acceptable air quality, though some pollutants may concern a small group. For instance, ozone-sensitive individuals may experience mild respiratory symptoms.

Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (101-150): Sensitive individuals might experience health effects. Those with lung or heart conditions face higher risks, but the general public is less likely to be affected.

Unhealthy (151-200): General health effects may emerge, with sensitive groups experiencing more serious issues.

Very Unhealthy (201-300): A health alert triggers as serious health effects become more widespread.

Hazardous (300 and above): Brace for health warnings of emergency conditions, impacting the entire population.

Curious for more details? Explore the US EPA AQI classifications here. Your guide to breathing well starts with knowledge!

How to Calculate AQI

Ever wondered how we gauge air quality? The Air Quality Index (AQI) is your key, calculated for five pivotal air pollutants: SO2, CO, NO2, PM10, PM2.5, and O3.

Here's the scoop:

Particulate Matter

Particulate matter (PM) takes the international stage as a priority pollutant, impacting public health, ecosystems, materials, and climate. It's a key player in low visibility incidents.PM stems from varied sources—natural materials' transportation, construction dust, traffic, and industrial activities. Secondary particles emerge from atmospheric reactions, like the photochemical oxidation of sulphur dioxide.Airborne particles vary in size. Larger ones settle on surfaces, causing nuisances. However, health concerns revolve around fine particulate matter, specifically PM10 and PM2.5, with the potential to penetrate deep into the respiratory system, triggering a range of health issues.

Nitrogen Dioxide

Every combustion process generates nitrogen oxides, and among them, nitrogen dioxide (NO2) takes centre stage. It is linked to adverse human health effects, primarily stemming from the atmospheric oxidation of nitric oxide—produced during the combustion of hydrocarbon fuels. Key emission sources include road traffic, power stations, and industrial activities. NO2 is not just a statistic; it can directly impact the respiratory system. Inhalation can lead to inflammation of the airways, posing a significant health risk. Particularly vulnerable are individuals with asthma or pre-existing respiratory conditions, as they can experience adverse effects even at low concentrations.

Sulphur Dioxide

Sulphur dioxide (SO2) emerges as a byproduct of burning fuel and processing materials with sulphur contaminants. Common sources encompass oil-fired power stations, oil and gas processing, industrial activities, and road traffic.Inhaling sulphur dioxide takes a toll on the respiratory system, causing airway constriction and breathing difficulties. This impact is particularly pronounced in individuals with asthma and chronic lung disease. SO2's effects extend beyond the lungs. It plays a role in the formation of acid rain, triggering widespread ecosystem damage in rivers and lakes, even thousands of kilometres away from the initial emission source. Vegetation is not spared either—direct or indirect impacts occur at concentrations lower than those affecting human health.

Carbon Monoxide

Carbon monoxide (CO) may be invisible and odourless, but its impact is undeniable. It arises from the incomplete combustion of hydrocarbon fuels, with road traffic, especially petrol-engine vehicles, standing out as a primary source. The real danger lies in the formation of carboxyhaemoglobin in the blood, significantly impeding the uptake and transport of oxygen in the body. The threat is particularly acute for individuals with pre-existing conditions that affect oxygen delivery to vital organs, such as coronary artery disease.

Ozone

Ozone (O3) isn't just present; it's produced in the atmosphere through intricate chemical reactions involving nitrogen oxides, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), and the presence of sunlight. These reactions unfold over hours or days, depending on the VOCs in the mix. Surprisingly, the ozone measured at a specific spot may have originated from precursor compounds produced hundreds or even thousands of kilometres away. Once born, ozone journeys with the wind, influenced by meteorological conditions. Maximum concentrations often emerge at a distance downwind from the source emissions. In bustling urban areas with high traffic, ozone faces a dance of reduction. Interactions with nitric oxide lead to a transformation, resulting in the formation of nitrogen dioxide. The urban hustle and bustle play a role in modifying ozone concentrations. Ozone's impact transcends the air we breathe. It's a silent assailant to plants, inducing leaf necrosis and affecting photosynthesis. This, in turn, hampers the production of crops and trees.

Volatile Organic Compounds

Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) isn't just a term; it's a vast realm of chemical diversity. Encompassing aliphatic, aromatic, and halogenated hydrocarbons, along with aldehydes, ketones, esters, and amines, VOCs paint a rich chemical canvas. The impact isn't limited to the atmosphere. VOCs have a say in human health, with some species directly posing toxicity risks. Additionally, certain VOCs unleash offensive odours even at remarkably low concentrations.
